## 🎮 Two Truths and a Tech Lie

**Best for:** Session 1  
**Time:** 5–8 minutes  
**Materials:** None

**How it works:**
1. Each person thinks of **two true statements** and **one false statement** (the "lie") about themselves — with at least one statement related to technology.
2. They share all three, and the group guesses which one is the lie.

**Example:**
- "I use Snapchat every day." (true)
- "I've never watched a YouTube tutorial." (lie)
- "My mum works at a bank." (true)

**Why it works:** Low-pressure, naturally funny, and reveals that everyone has a tech life already. Good segue into discussing how tech is already part of their lives.

**Facilitation tip:** Model it yourself first to lower the barrier.

## 🎮 Human Bingo

**Best for:** Session 1 (large group, first-ever meeting)  
**Time:** 8–10 minutes  
**Materials:** Printed bingo cards (one per student)

**How it works:**
Students walk around the room and find classmates who match a description in each bingo square. When someone signs a square, write their name in it. First to fill a row shouts "BINGO!"

**Sample Tech-Themed Bingo Card:**

```
┌────────────────────┬────────────────────┬────────────────────┐
│ Has made a video   │ Uses more than 3   │ Has tried coding   │
│ before             │ different apps     │ at least once      │
│                    │ today              │                    │
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┼────────────────────┤
│ Knows what HTML    │ Has a sibling or   │ Has watched a      │
│ stands for         │ cousin who works   │ YouTube tutorial   │
│                    │ in tech            │ to learn something │
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┼────────────────────┤
│ Has used Google    │ Can name 3 Kenyan  │ Has an idea for    │
│ Docs to do         │ tech companies     │ an app they'd      │
│ schoolwork         │                    │ love to build      │
└────────────────────┴────────────────────┴────────────────────┘
```

**Facilitation tip:** Debrief by asking: *"Who found someone who'd tried coding? What did they build?"*

## 🎮 Dream Job Bingo

**Best for:** Session 2 (Career Conversations — before speakers arrive)  
**Time:** 8–10 minutes  
**Materials:** Printed bingo cards

**How it works:** Same as Human Bingo, but cards are career-themed.

**Sample Career-Themed Bingo Card:**

## 🎮 Would You Rather (Tech Edition)

**Best for:** Session 2  
**Time:** 5 minutes  
**Materials:** None

**How it works:**
Facilitator calls out two options. Students move to one side of the room for their choice. No wrong answers.

**Sample questions:**
1. Would you rather be a **game developer** or a **doctor who uses technology to save lives**?
2. Would you rather work at a **startup** (fast, small team, risky) or **Big Tech** (Google/Safaricom — stable, large)?
3. Would you rather **design how an app looks** or **build the engine that makes it work**?
4. Would you rather build something that **helps 10 people deeply** or **helps 10,000 people a little**?
5. Would you rather work **from home** or **in an office with a team**?

**Facilitation tip:** After each question, ask one student on each side to defend their choice in 10 seconds. Good primer for the panel discussion.

## 🎮 Tech Trivia Lightning Round

**Best for:** Session 3 (or any session as a warm-up)  
**Time:** 8–10 minutes  
**Materials:** Questions prepared (read aloud); small prize for winner optional

**How it works:**
Ask questions, first hand up gets to answer. Keep score on the whiteboard. 

**Sample Questions:**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What does HTML stand for? | HyperText Markup Language |
| 2 | Which country created the programming language Python? | Netherlands (Guido van Rossum, Dutch) |
| 3 | What does "bug" mean in programming? | An error or flaw in the code |
| 4 | What Kenyan mobile payment system transformed how people send money? | M-Pesa |
| 5 | What company owns Instagram, WhatsApp, and Facebook? | Meta |
| 6 | What does AI stand for? | Artificial Intelligence |
| 7 | Who is often called the "first computer programmer"? | Ada Lovelace |
| 8 | What does "open source" mean? | Software whose code is publicly available for anyone to use/modify |
| 9 | What is the name of the free coding platform where many beginners start? | Scratch (or accept: freeCodeCamp, CodePen) |
| 10 | What programming language is most commonly used for websites? | JavaScript |

## 🎮 My Superpower in a Team

**Best for:** Session 3 (before group formation — helps students self-identify strengths)  
**Time:** 5–8 minutes  
**Materials:** Sticky notes, pens

**How it works:**
1. Each student writes their **one biggest strength** in a team on a sticky note.
   - Prompt: *"What do people always come to you for? What are you good at that helps a group?"*
   - Examples: "I'm good at organising." / "I'm creative." / "I calm people down when stressed." / "I never give up."
2. Students share in pairs first, then a few share with the room.
3. Facilitator names the diversity in the room: *"We have organisers, creatives, problem-solvers — a great team needs all of these."*

**Why it works:** Validates non-technical strengths and primes students to think about team roles before group formation.

## 🎮 Emoji Check-In

**Best for:** Any session (especially as a quick opener or after a break)  
**Time:** 2–3 minutes  
**Materials:** Whiteboard or projector

**How it works:**
Facilitator draws or displays 5 emojis:
- 🚀 "Excited and ready to go"
- 😊 "Feeling good"
- 😐 "It's okay"
- 😴 "Tired / could use energy"
- 😕 "Confused / lost"

Students either shout out their emoji, hold up fingers (1–5), or vote on a screen.

**Why it works:** Quick, low-stakes, builds psychological safety. Helps facilitator calibrate energy and pace.

## 🎮 The Name & Motion Game

**Best for:** First time a new group meets (any session)  
**Time:** 5 minutes  
**Materials:** None

**How it works:**
1. Everyone stands in a circle.
2. First person says their name AND makes a simple motion (e.g., claps, spins, jumps).
3. The whole group repeats the name and motion.
4. Next person does theirs, and the group repeats all names so far.
5. Continue until everyone has gone.

**Facilitation tip:** Go fast to keep it fun. Laugh when people get confused — that's the point!

## 🎮 2-Minute Tech Sketch

**Best for:** Any session (good after a long listening segment)  
**Time:** 5 minutes  
**Materials:** Paper, pens (one per student)

**How it works:**
1. Facilitator gives a prompt: *"Draw the app you would build if you had unlimited time and skill."*
2. Students have 2 minutes to sketch (no judgement — stick figures are fine).
3. Students share their sketch with the person next to them in 30 seconds each.
4. Optionally, invite 2–3 people to show the room.

**Why it works:** Unlocks creativity; shifts energy; surfaces project ideas naturally.

## General Facilitation Tips for Icebreakers

- **Always model the activity yourself first.** Students are less likely to take risks if you don't.
- **Keep it moving.** A good icebreaker should feel slightly rushed — that's the energy.
- **Don't force participation.** Let students observe first if they're shy; they'll join in when they feel safe.
- **Connect the icebreaker to the session theme.** A brief "bridge" sentence helps students understand why you did the activity.
- **Prizes are optional but fun.** Even a sweet or a sticker can create a great moment.
