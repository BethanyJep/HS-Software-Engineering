# Facilitator Guide

A comprehensive guide for TOFA members, Alliance Girls Alumni, and anyone facilitating the Alliance Girls High School Software Engineering Program.

---

## Welcome, Facilitator!

Thank you for volunteering your time to make this program possible. You are directly shaping what these students think is possible for their futures. That matters enormously.

This guide will help you prepare for each session, manage the unexpected, and create an environment where every student feels they belong in tech.

## Facilitator Principles

### 1. You don't need to know everything.
You are not a professor — you are a guide. If a student asks a question you can't answer, model intellectual curiosity:
> *"That's a brilliant question. I don't know the answer off the top of my head — let's find out together."*

### 2. Representation matters more than perfection.
Your presence as an African woman who works in tech (or who cares about tech education) is itself the message. You don't need a perfect lesson to make an impact.

### 3. Every student belongs.
Some students will walk in thinking *"this isn't for me."* Your job is to dismantle that belief gently but persistently. Watch for students who seem disengaged or intimidated — a small, quiet word of encouragement can change everything.

### 4. Mistakes are part of the method.
When demos break, when code doesn't work, when speakers arrive late — model calm problem-solving. Show students what it looks like to adapt.

### 5. Ask, don't tell.
Wherever possible, ask questions rather than giving answers. This builds critical thinking and makes students feel heard.

- [ ] Arrive 20–30 minutes early

- [ ] Arrange seating (tables for groups are better than theatre-style rows)
- [ ] Have water available (for you and for speakers)
- [ ] Greet students at the door — energy is set before they sit down

## Managing the Classroom

### Energising a low-energy group
- Change the activity format (stand up, move around).
- Use an icebreaker or energiser (see [Icebreakers](icebreakers.md)).
- Give a short break (5 minutes).
- Lower the threshold: *"Just try — there are no wrong answers."*

### Managing a dominant student
- Acknowledge their contribution, then redirect: *"Great point — who else has a thought?"*
- Privately, after class, channel their energy: *"I'd love for you to help bring others into the conversation."*

### Handling the "I can't do this" student
- Never dismiss it — validate first: *"I hear you. This stuff can feel overwhelming."*
- Pair them with a supportive peer.
- Find one tiny win they can celebrate.
- Share a personal story of your own confusion when starting out.

### Managing time
- Have a co-facilitator track time and signal when 5 minutes remain.
- It's okay to cut an activity short — say *"We're going to pause there — more on that next time."*
- Never rush the icebreaker or Q&A — those are often where the real learning happens.

## Running Guest Speakers (Session 2)

### Before the session
- Share the [Speaker Briefing](../term2/session-2-career-conversations.md#before-the-session--speaker-preparation) with speakers at least one week in advance.
- Have a 10-minute prep call if possible.
- Collect speaker bios to introduce them confidently.

### During the session
- Set expectations before speakers present: *"This person has agreed to be honest and real with you — please treat their story with respect."*
- Keep time firmly — give a 2-minute warning.
- Moderate Q&A to ensure everyone gets a chance to ask.
- If a question is inappropriate or puts a speaker on the spot, step in: *"That's a big question — let me rephrase it."*

### After the session
- Thank speakers (publicly in the room and follow up with a personal thank-you).
- Collect any resources they promised to share.
- Brief the team on themes to carry into subsequent sessions.

## Supporting Term 3 Project Teams

During Term 3, your role shifts from facilitator to **coach and mentor**.

### What good coaching looks like
- Ask questions, don't give answers: *"What have you tried? What happened? What might you try next?"*
- Celebrate small progress: *"You got that button working — that's real progress."*
- Help teams prioritise: *"If you could only finish ONE thing this week, what would it be?"*
- Be honest about scope: *"This is a great idea for version 2 — let's make version 1 work first."*

### Dealing with common team problems

| Problem | Coaching approach |
|---|---|
| One person is doing all the work | Facilitate a role conversation: *"How can you split this more evenly?"* |
| Team has a conflict | Don't take sides. Remind them of the goal: *"What would your user need you to do right now?"* |
| Team wants to change their idea mid-sprint | Validate the instinct, but ask: *"How much of what you've built can be reused?"* |
| Team is stuck on a technical problem for days | First, encourage them to search and try independently. Then step in with hints, not solutions. |
| Team is demotivated | Show them what they HAVE built. Find an external person to give positive feedback. |

## The Showcase – Running the Prize Ceremony

### Setup
- Arrange the room in a presentation format (audience facing a stage / front area).
- Each team gets a table or space to set up their demo.
- Have a judging panel of 3–5 people (alumni, industry guests, a teacher).
- Share judging criteria with judges in advance (see [Project Guidelines](../term3/project-guidelines.md#judging-criteria)).

### Running Order
1. Opening remarks (5 min) — acknowledge the students' effort; set a celebratory tone.
2. Team presentations (5–7 min each + 3 min Q&A).
3. Break while judges deliberate (10–15 min) — use this time for informal demos and photos.
4. Awards & Prize Ceremony.
5. Closing remarks — thank facilitators, sponsors, teachers, and students.

### Award Categories (suggested)
- 🥇 **Best Overall Project** — top-scoring team by judging criteria
- 🎨 **Best Design** — most user-friendly and visually appealing solution
- 💡 **Most Creative Idea** — most original problem or approach
- 🛠️ **Best Technical Execution** — most functional and technically impressive
- ❤️ **Most Impactful** — addresses the most significant community need

> **Note:** Giving multiple categories means more teams are celebrated, which builds a positive culture for the program's next cohort.

## Self-Care for Facilitators

Facilitation is rewarding but tiring. A few reminders:

- It's okay if a session doesn't go perfectly. Reflect, adjust, and move on.
- Debrief with your co-facilitators after each session — share wins and challenges.
- You're a volunteer. Ask for help if the workload feels too heavy.
- The students will remember how you made them feel more than what you said.
